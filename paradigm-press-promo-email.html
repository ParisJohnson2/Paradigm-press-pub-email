Your September Strategic Intelligence Portfolio Update

DAN
AMOSS

Dear Strategic Intelligence Reader,

In the September briefing, Jim discussed the recent BRICS+ meeting, the Fed's current policy stance, and the strong likelihood that investors are way too confident about the U.S. economy as well as the stability of banks across the country.

If you missed the briefing or want to review it, please click here for access.

Near the end of the briefing, Jim explained how changes in consumption and inventories drove recent optimism about the GDP numbers.

These are temporary factors. They can reverse quickly.

Investors have mistaken a lagged economic response to Fed rate hikes for the avoidance of a recession.

But a recession is on the way.

With the U.S. economy so influenced by debt and asset prices, consumer sentiment can go from red hot to ice cold within months.

Coincident economic indicators still look fine.

Leading economic indicators do not.

After looking back several decades, to the 1960s, a recession has followed six out of six times when credit conditions (as measured by the Fed's Senior Loan Officer Survey) have been this tight.

See the implication of this survey for future loan growth in the chart below.

Source: Apollo's Chief Economist

The chart shows that we have every reason to expect consumer and industrial (C&I) loan growth to shrink over the next year at a rate only seen during the 2008 crisis and during the Covid lockdowns!

What's the state of investor sentiment with a bank credit crunch on the horizon?

In short, investment bank surveys, fund flows, and media attention all confirm that almost every investor is overexposed to tech stocks at a time when they're vulnerable to a long-delayed recession.

The lack of fund flows, attention, and cheap valuations in energy stocks confirm that opportunities are plentiful in this area of the market.

Below are our latest thoughts on all open portfolio positions.

In preview, little has changed since there has not been much impactful macro or earnings-related news. The most notable company-level news was from Crescent Energy (CRGY), which reported solid earnings in mid-August and announced a smart acquisition in September.

As we approach Q3 earnings season from mid-October to mid-November, we expect to see more earnings warnings that rain on bullish investor sentiment. The year-end rush may be more of a rush for hedge funds and financial advisors to de-risk portfolios than a rush to buy.

The September 1 issue included Jim's latest view on the risk of an economic collapse in China. If that happens, we'd see a risk-off reallocation from the ocean of the $200 trillion+ global stock and corporate bond market to the lake of $4 trillion in long-duration Treasury bonds.

That would push Treasury prices much higher, so we recommended the PIMCO 25+ Year Zero Coupon U.S. Treasury ETF (NYSE: ZROZ).

We are interested in a trade with a 3–12-month time horizon here. Catalysts for a ZROZ rally include a global recession, with a potential China collapse to kick a recession into hyperdrive.

The PIMCO 25+ Year Zero Coupon U.S. Treasury ETF (NYSE: ZROZ) is a buy up to $90 per share.

Wheaton Precious Metals

On August 3, we recommended Wheaton Precious Metals (NYSE: WPM) is one of the top gold and silver streaming companies in the world.

Based on the company’s successful track record, we are confident that Wheaton will invest its cash hoard and its incoming cash flow back into high-return streams far into the future.

We think that a business of this quality is worth 25 times cash flow.

Assuming higher gold and silver prices, plus higher production as new streams come online, we see a path to $2 billion in cash flow by 2025. That should push WPM well above $100 per share. How soon WPM gets there depends mostly on the future path of gold and silver prices.

Wheaton Precious Metals (NYSE: WPM) is a buy up to $52 per share.

Baxter International

In the July 5 issue, we recommended Baxter International (NYSE: BAX), a producer of medical products in these categories: surgical, diagnostic, critical care, kidney care, and nutrition. Its products are consumed in nursing homes, hospitals, and doctors’ offices.

Healthcare is one of the most predictable, defensive sectors in the market. Perhaps the most attractive feature of Baxter’s product portfolio is its exposure to many applications that will grow as the U.S. population ages.

At today’s low valuation, management is under serious pressure to hit earnings targets. If the stock remains cheap, it could be acquired by a larger peer.

On July 27, Baxter reported solid Q2 results. Sales were ahead of management’s guidance, driven by the ongoing recovery in patient and procedure volumes. The fastest product line sales growth was in Front Line Care, Surgical Solutions, Medication Delivery, and Clinical Nutrition.

Although management issued decent guidance for the second half and is executing well, the stock has traded lower since the report. If you don’t already have a position, BAX is an interesting value stock play on a recovery in healthcare system activity.

Baxter International (NYSE: BAX) is a buy up to $52 per share.
